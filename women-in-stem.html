Women in STEM

  • Prarthana Madan
  • Sept 9, 2021

We live in a world where fields associated with technology are majorly male-dominated. Even today STEM is broadly associated with men. For years women have been silenced and told that tech is not for them. According to a recent survey, women’s share in STEM jobs in India is just 14 per cent. Studies revealed that when kindergarteners were asked to draw a scientist (DAST- originally developed by David wade Chambers in 1983), most of them perceived them to be men. Very few drawings portrayed women and those were made by the girls. Those women who are successfully working in this field, often experience workplace discrimination and are more likely to be paid less by their male counterparts.

But why does this gender disparity exist? This is due to several reasons tying to a predominantly masculine culture that stereotypically statesmen are “more fitted” for STEM jobs. Due to math anxiety and lack of confidence introduced at a very young age, women often tend to underestimate their abilities. There are still households in this country that tell their daughters that women are made to serve their families. Girls from a very young age are indirectly or directly forced to associate a picture of an engineer with a man. In several corners of the world, women were and still are told that being smart doesn’t attract, even if that's the last thing they’d want to do. There are very few yet extremely precious role models who have broken through this huge gender barrier. The stereotypic perspective towards STEM is highlighted by Nancy Roman, the famous American astronomer’s personal experience in school, she said“I still remember asking my high school guidance teacher for permission to require a second year of algebra rather than a fifth year of Latin. She looked down her nose at me and sneered, 'What lady would take mathematics rather than Latin?”

It's about time we change the way the world sees women in STEM. All the young girls out there aspiring to pursue a STEM career, we’d like you to know that we are here for you. No matter what the world tells you, don’t let them ruin the dream in your eyes. As Emilie du Chatelet once said, and I quote "Let us choose for ourselves our path in life, and let us try to strew that path with flowers." Yes, it might be difficult to combat this stereotype which lingers in our society, but we need all the girls to know that they have people to support them in every little step they take. Those people might not be around you all the time, but they are always with you. All one needs to do is get online and search for communities around them that will help them grow in the STEM field. And even though the number might be small, we have some marvellous STEM heroines to look up to.

Gitanjali Rao

She is a fifteen-year-old innovator, author and STEM promoter. She is also the innovator behind Tethys which is a device used to detect lead in drinking water. She was given the title of TIME’s first-ever kid of the year in 2020

Alyssa Carson(nasablueberry)

Carson has attended space camp 7 times and space academy 3 times. The 19-year-old is the youngest to graduate from Advanced Space Academy. In January 2013, NASA invited her to be on the MER 10 panel in Washington DC to discuss future missions to Mars live on NASA TV.

Dr. Ritu Karidhal

Dr. Ritu Karidhal is a scientist working at ISRO who was the Deputy Operations Director to India's Mars Orbital Mission, Mangalyaan.

Mary W. Jackson

She was NASA’s first female African American engineer who worked at NASA for 34 years and was posthumously awarded the Congressional Gold Medal, in 2019.


These are just a few of the many phenomenal women who have shaped and continue to shape the future of women in STEM. Our program Code with Her, is a place where one can augment their vocational skills including STEM ones. Under this program, we’ll conduct workshops for girls, by girls. It’s an attempt to bridge the gap between men and women in STEM and many other fields.

Prarthana Madan
